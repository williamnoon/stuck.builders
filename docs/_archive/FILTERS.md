# THE FOUR FILTERS — Will's Lens

*Source of truth for how the four filters get applied — inside the Idea Sprint, and on every sprint application. Pairs with PRD.md §6.*

---

## 1. Purpose

The four filters do two jobs. First, they're the **pressure-test framework** applied to ideas inside the Idea Sprint — Day 1 runs the idea through all four, and the failures become the week's findings. Second, they're the **acceptance lens** Will uses on every sprint application before a slot is offered. Filter 1 is a hard gate: fail it and the project is declined, no discussion. Filters 2–4 are coaching judgment — held loosely, weighed together, applied by a human who's read the application. This is not a rubric. It's a lens.

---

## 2. Filter 1 — Hard Line (auto-reject)

**Not illegal. Not harmful. Not predatory.**

This is the only automatic filter. If a project trips it, the application is declined the same day with a clean, non-judgmental note. Money doesn't change hands. Will doesn't coach it.

Auto-reject examples:
- MLM / pyramid / recruitment-comp schemes
- Dark-pattern engagement products (rage-bait, addiction loops targeting kids, manipulative retention mechanics)
- Deceptive financial products (fake yield, hidden fees, "guaranteed returns," pig-butchering-adjacent)
- Weapons, illegal drugs, unlicensed firearms accessories
- Adult content that targets or exposes minors
- Unlicensed medical, legal, or financial advice sold as a product
- Any project where the customer clearly benefits at another party's expense — predatory by construction

**The test for predatory:** if the business model requires someone to lose for the customer to win, and that loser didn't sign up for the game, it fails.

Will doesn't argue this filter. It's stated on the application form so people self-select out before applying.

---

## 3. Filter 2 — Real Problem (coaching judgment)

**Not a problem invented to justify the idea.**

The most common failure mode of first-time builders: fall in love with a solution, then reverse-engineer a problem to point it at. Filter 2 catches that.

Signals it's a **real** problem:
- People bump into it in daily life — not once, repeatedly
- Multiple independent complaints in threads, reviews, subreddits, support tickets
- Workarounds already exist (spreadsheets, Zapier duct tape, a group chat, a Notion doc)
- Someone already pays money or spends real time to solve it today
- The applicant can name three specific people (by name, not persona) who've hit it

Signals it's **invented**:
- The applicant can't name anyone else who's asked for it
- The pain is theoretical — "people probably struggle with…"
- Every alternative framing starts with "well, if you assume that…"
- The problem statement grows or shrinks to fit whatever the solution happens to do

This one gets pressure-tested hard in the Idea Sprint. If the applicant can't produce evidence by Day 3, that's the finding.

---

## 4. Filter 3 — Valuable / Beneficial (coaching judgment)

**Deserves someone's time and attention. Makes life better, not just busier.**

A problem can be real and still not worth solving. Filter 3 asks whether the solution earns its place in someone's day.

Signals it's **valuable**:
- Solving it saves the user time, money, or measurable pain
- Or it unlocks a specific enjoyable outcome the user can name
- The user, if it worked, would tell a friend without being asked
- It respects the user's attention rather than farming it

Anti-signals:
- Attention farming — the product only "wins" by keeping people scrolling
- Novelty without utility — cool demo, no repeat use
- "Cool but why" — the applicant can't finish the sentence "this matters because…"
- The value accrues to the operator (data, engagement, ads) more than to the user

Filter 3 is where Will decides whether he can stand behind the thing publicly. If he'd be embarrassed to name it as a project he coached, it fails.

---

## 5. Filter 4 — Close to You (coaching judgment)

**You have background, interest, or lived proximity to this problem.**

The best version of an idea has the builder's fingerprints on it. Someone who's been the customer, worked in the industry, or has a real personal stake will ship a better product than someone chasing a market they read about.

Signals of **closeness**:
- The applicant has been the customer of this problem
- They've worked in the domain — day job, side project, prior company
- They have strong personal interest, hobby-level depth, or family/community proximity
- They can describe the problem's texture — specific moments, specific frustrations

Anti-signals:
- "I just think this would be a good market"
- No personal connection, only a market-size argument
- Idea sourced from a Twitter thread or a trend report with no lived hook
- The applicant can't say why *they* are the right person to build this

Filter 4 isn't gatekeeping who's "allowed" to build in a space — it's a diagnostic. When closeness is missing, the Idea Sprint's Day 1 background-mining often uncovers a better, closer idea sitting right next to the one they applied with.

---

## 6. Application-Review Protocol

Will follows this on every application. Reply within **24 hours** either way.

1. **Read Q1–Q5 answers** (and the link/attachment if provided).
2. **Filter 1 check — 5 seconds.** If it fails, send the clean decline template. Done.
3. **Filters 2–4 as a gestalt.** Not scored. Read together.
   - All three green or amber → **accept**. Send payment link + booking.
   - Two or more red → **decline-wrong-fit**. Route them to a lower-commit option:
     - Applied for BUILD with no signal (nothing actually built yet)? → suggest the $199 Idea Sprint.
     - Applied for BUILD (Traction, `/grow`) with nothing shipped? → suggest core Build Sprint first (`/` or `/out`), or Idea Sprint if nothing built.
     - Idea is early and closeness is weak? → "come back after you've talked to 5 potential users," or suggest Skool as a lower-commit way to move at group pace.
4. **When in doubt, accept for the Idea Sprint, decline for BUILD.** Idea Sprint exists to answer the exact questions Filters 2–4 pose; Build assumes they're already green and something exists to work on.

The reply is short, direct, and never condescending. A declined applicant should walk away knowing exactly why and what to do next.

---

## 7. Applied Inside the Idea Sprint

Day 1 is the pressure-test. All four filters run explicitly against the applicant's idea, out loud, in the ~30-min Day 1 call.

- **Filter 1** — usually cleared at application. If something new surfaces here, the sprint ends and the fee is refunded.
- **Filter 2** — becomes the entire Days 2–5 investigation. Real customer conversations. Real threads. Real workarounds. If Filter 2 doesn't hold up by Day 5, the Day 6 decision is *pivot or don't build*.
- **Filter 3** — surfaces in customer conversations too. If nobody a real customer describes the outcome as valuable, that's a Filter 3 fail.
- **Filter 4** — anchored on Day 1 during background-mining. When the applied-with idea is far from the builder but a closer, better idea shows up in their background, the sprint pivots. That's a feature, not a failure.

An honest "don't build this, here's what to build instead" is a completed Idea Sprint.

---

## 8. What the Filters Are NOT

- **Not a scorecard.** No points, no weights, no thresholds.
- **Not a rubric.** Two applications with the same signals can get different answers based on context.
- **Not automated.** No form logic decides acceptance. A human reads every application.
- **Not a marketing gimmick.** They exist because Will only works on projects he can stand behind — and that's what makes the money-back posture possible. If he wouldn't put his name on it, he doesn't take it.

The filters aren't there to seem selective. They're there so the guarantee is real.
